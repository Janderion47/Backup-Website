I have had the opportunity recently to put into place some of the ideas of our dealings with the treatment of wrongdoing here in philosophy. I have been leading a Dungeons & Dragons game for the past few weeks, and it has recently became important that I establish the justice system of the fictional setting of our game. Considering the cultural context of the world, I decided that the justice system would be structured around the idea of rehabilitating the minds of criminals as it seems appropriate to implement in the society. The important question in doing this task is how should approach the creation of a rehabilitating system that respects people’s status as rational beings and respects peoples personal autonomy, which the latter is a concern of Kant on retributive.

Actually, I want to approach this critique of retribution's push to change the minds of people. I will say, if attempting to convince someone that their current form of thinking is an infringement of a person’s personal autonomy, then attempting to convince a person to change their mind of anything is an infringement, which completely disassembles our ideas of how we should be open to other ideas and arguments made by other people. How can we have any discussions if any attempt to convince a person of something is wrong? I argue that a sufficient condition of an action being an infringement on a person’s personal autonomy is if the action was forcing upon this individual something that is actually going against their will because it would seem correct to equate one’s personal autonomy with their will over themselves.


I made a joke to my friend Jessica, who is one of the players in my D&D game, that there was a ten step program for the rehabilitation of criminals in the setting’s justice system. I then decided from that one small piece of information that I would create this plan. Going back to the question that I asked earlier here, I think that to create this system that would rehabilitate people whilst also respecting their status as rational beings and their autonomy would be to make it as if we were trying to convince someone of something; and since we are engaged here in logical thinking, I think the best way to do this would be to basically show them the structure of our logic and argument that what they have done is wrong, hoping that they will agree with our argument, or if not, that they would provide reasoning as to why they disagree as to our argument. This reflects our basic way of presenting arguments to other people, that we present the argument, and the other party will have the chance to support or refute the argument, and in all cases, we can always provide supplementary arguments. After this deliberation, we should then present again, perhaps the errors in their logic, and how to correct those errors, which essentially is teaching them how to be better people.
