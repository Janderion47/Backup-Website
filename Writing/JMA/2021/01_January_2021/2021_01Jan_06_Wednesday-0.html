Good Father?

I once watched a television episode which featured a scene where a man was going to leave his wife and daughter to protest/fight to promote for the increasing of government measures to combat climate change. The man said that he would not return until he had ensured that the world will be set on a track of improving the conditions, he wanted his daughter to be able to live in a world where she does not have to worry about the climate of the world which I believe is a noble cause. What I want to question is if this noble cause outweighs the daughter’s need for her father?

The man, actually I do not want to keep saying that, lets call him Joe; Joe certainly values his daughter as I specifically remember that he wants her future to be as best as it can, but it seems that he ignores her need for a father in her life, sort of valuing her average comfort in life over her average comfort in life in the shorter term, which if this is a math equation in the form of ‘a’ is greater than ‘b’, then I would agree in Joe’s decision to maximize the value of ‘a’. I can understand this as parents want the best for their childre; its like self sacrifice, I am sure that there have been numerous scenarios depicted in movies and t.v. series that show a parent sacrifising their own life for the survival of their children; Joe, in not participating in his daugther’s childhood, is practically dead in her life.

Is Joe a good father in this case? Well, does valuing his daughter’s future outweigh her need for a father in her life? We’re gonna need a definition for “a good father” if we’re going to attack this quesiton, as I have listened to a podcast where a group of friends were playing the tabletop game Dungeons & Dragons, which is a roleplay, improv game in which multiple people (known as the players) roleplay specific characters (known as playable characters or PCs) and one of the members of the game controls the setting and plot of the story of the game as it goes on (known as the Dungeon Master or DM), the DM also roleplays non-playable character (NPCs). One of the recent episodes sees one of the PCs put on trial on accusations of being a bad father, which creates much interesting and funny discourse as the other PCs taking on the defense of their accused friend, and a guest player roleplays the offending lawyer. In roleplaying this court of law, the defending lawyers, began an attempt to use philosophical thinking in creating a defense for their friend, saying that, they acknowledge some of the legitimate claimes of bad parenting from their friend, the defendant’s intentions in trying to be a single father are good, and that the court of law cannot put him in jail for having good intentions but bad execution. The offending lawyer stated that it does not matter what the morals say, what matters within their court of law is the word of the law itself, questioning “by our law, has your friend broken our law?”. By this time, the players and DM had paused for a moment in their playing to acknowledge that the DM did not foresee the usage of morality, and that he was sorry for not specifying how the accused’s “goodness” would be judged. In the end, the accused was found guilty of being a bad dad, and the players found that it would be a magical embodiment of the law of the land that would eventually hand out the punishment to the character.

This episode of the podcast always brings me back to the fact that it is difficult to objectively determine what consitutes as being a good parent, as the “objective” perspective of the law in that podcast did not take into account the situationalness of events and incidents. Though, I think it would not be hard to consider what would consitute a good parent in terms of necessary and sufficient conditions; I think it would be harder to determine sufficient conditions for being a good parent than determining necessary conditions for the same as that suggest that there must be one singular trait of a person that would label them as a good parent. Even if we were to construct a list of necessary and sufficient conditions, I doubt that all people who might be considered good parents would be in accordance with all of the necessary conditions. Would we even be able to answer the question “Is Joe a good father in his decision to leave his family for the benefit of his daughter?” if there is no objective “good father” or “good parent”? If we cannot, then what is the significance of the fact that we have no objective definition for either?

I do not think that we would be able to answer the question as having an understanding of what a good father is necessary before we can compare Joe to the criteria. The fact that we have no objective definition shows that perhaps the concept of a good father is very much subjective, everyone can have their own opinion, but there must be a singlular definition, there must be.

Good and Evil

Thinking about this previous topic reminds me of one other topic discussing the idea of good. I remember the following ethical issue from the Marvel movie “Doctor Strange”, of which one of the central characters of the story had been revealed to use a form of magic considered to be evil, to power their good intentions toward the world. This was revealed when this central character had to face off with some actual forces of evil, who were also using the evil form of magic. Now, I was about to argue that, perhaps its not the magic that is evil, it is simply the people who use it are usually evil, considering that this central character is using it with good intentions, but for context, this magic comes from the literal enemy of the central character, which makes the central character a hypocrite as was called out by the one central evil character.

I will agree that this usage of the by-definition, evil magic by the central character makes her a hypocrate, as her entire school of magic was establish to protect the world from outside forces such as the one which the evil magic comes from, but does that make her a bad/evil person? That again needs a solid, objective definition of good apart from evil.

I think that it is also important to understand the diction of some of this idea that this central person is a hypocrite for posing as a good character yet using the weapons of evil depend on the idea that the use of said evil weapons is a necessary condition for being considered as evil. But if this previous idea is states that users are evil because they use evil weapons, the central character would then be evil by definition, yet is clearly not evil as seen in the movie. Now, there were actual “evil” characters in the movie, who were greatly opposed to the central character and the protagonist. The lead “evil” character critiqued the central character at the beginning of the movie, in a fight scene, where the central character had fought off many of the “evil” characters, whilst using the weapon of evil. The lead “evil” character called the central character a hypocrite as the central character had pledged to protect the earth from the great source of evil of which the weapon of evil.

If one can use of an instrument of evil, of which gives the user the status as being evil, but does not make this user evil, is it evil? I do not think that the instrument is evil then. Actually, (drawing away from the previously mentioned movie,) when I think of many systems of magic in many films, videogames and the such, there is not usually a solid definition of good or evil centered into the systems of magic themselves, more onto the socio-political structure of the society, and governments. For example, there may be a group of people who have excellent command over fire, and have fire related powers; this group might perform many political actions which would lead the group to be percieved as beign barbarian, a sort of evil, which this status may spread to the command of fire itself. But we, who look upon the political state of this fictional world can see that there is no innate “evilness” to the use of this fire. Another group may also command fire, but would be like switzerland, in being politically neutral and peaceful, they might be critiqued for their status as a neutral group and the ability to command fire, but nevertheless they are not evil; being able to command fire is not a necessary condition for being evil.

So if the weapons/tools/instruments of people do not define a person’s or a group’s status as a being good or evil, then what is able to determine status as good or evil? Well, lets think about political motivations. The Cold War featured the opposition of the two greatest powers of the world. Both sides deemed the other to be bad, while themselves are good. The American side claimed capitalism to be better than communism and the Soviet Union claimed that communism is better. But how can, for example, capitalism be both good and bad in comparison to communism? This is only a contradiction if status as being both good and evil is a sufficient condition for being a problem. But is it a problem? Well the United States’ appraisal of capitalism being valued higher than communism is motivation for actual violence as seen in history, with the USSR’s appraisal of communism is valued higher than capitalism does the same, perhaps a rephrasing of the two points of views in terms of higher or lower status would be much more clearer than simply stating that one is good and one is evil, but this assumes that in all cases, the status of good versus evil is dependent on two items being valued against each other and the one with the highest value being good, and the second would be bad. Considering this, it seems that there are two different comparisons here, good and evil as complete polar opposites, and things that are similar to a degree of which they must be compared. The former seems to be a scale of which one is either, nothing in the middle, but on one of the ends; while the latter is certainly a scale of which can lie anywhere between the two ends. The former suggests that things are either purely good or evil, the latter permits people to be some mix of the characteristics of a good or evil person. Both cases suggest that there is some sort of pure standard for both good and evil, though I do not think that it would be easy to judge an entitiy’s status as being good or evil if the only options are either pure good or pure evil; the entity that will be judged will be weighed against the standards.

I realize now that the main question that I would like to find and answer to is “Is there truly a philosophical good and evil?” I do not think that there would be any illogic in creating a standard of good and evil, the standard would be an example of how one should live what being a good or bad person is, what they do, but the basis of which we create this would then be asking a different question of how one should live.



About when we studied Aristotle’s four causes, I gave some thought to how we might develop our ethics, how it means to be a good person and what it means to be a bad person. But I considered that there are no innate things in the universe, as Aristotle imagined in the use of his four causes. This is something that I have considered for a long time, how we build our ethics using our knowledge of philosophy and logical thinking. I once came to the conclusion that there is no innate meaning to anything in existence and that all meaning and definitions have been artificially created by our human minds attempting to comprehend our own existence. If there is no innateness in the world, and all concepts are artificial, I believe that we will have to pick one statement or idea to be the central point of which all other decisions and ethics will be built from. I think that most societies in human history have used this previous structure of taking one idea as the central point for all of one’s thinking; for example, judaism/christianity/islam which has established the word of God as the focal point of their ethics and decisions, as God’s word has established what is good (at least in jews and christians, I am not too familiar with the faith of muslims); god, who is supreme over all, has determined what is good and evil.

Good has always seemed to be in reference to things of the world that follow a sort of , natural order, or “the natural order”, while evil/ bad does the opposite, and goes against the flow of the natural order. If we set this idea as our central point of which to build our ethics, then it suggests that all people that follow the natural order are good people.
