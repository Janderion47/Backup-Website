Part 1:

I am currently listening to the forty-ninth episode of the podcast “Dungeons & Daddies” and let’s just say that I have been waiting for their first episode of 2021, very eagerly. So far, I am about twenty-eight minutes in and, it is within my expectations let’s just say.

It is interesting to me how role play games make people face moral decisions. For those who do not know, in Dungeons and Dragons (DnD), the games are controlled by what is essentially the narrator, known as the Dungeon Master (DM), and basically, they have full control of the plot, the setting, and just generally can do whatever with the story as they see fit. The DM of Dungeons & Daddies, which I will now refer to as ‘DnDad’, is a guy named Anthony Burch, and one of the biggest plot points that he has placed in the story so far is [, without any context as to not spoil the story], when essentially everyone in the game had to decide if whether one of the playable characters (PC) is a good father to his son. After much deliberation, it was decided that the specific PC was a good person, but performed poorly as a father, and was therefore a bad father.

It is this situation which has led me to question if role play like this is an effective tool for teaching morality and letting people make decisions that would affect only people who were fake. Just like reading a book, more specifically, a novel of which we are able to follow some characters and we most likely become attached to some of them, especially if we are able to really see into their mind and how they think; it is that which I believe we are able to relate to characters as we may be able to compare ourselves to them and also critique their actions and thoughts. Doing this, we can learn some lessons that we possible would not be able to as perhaps the situations that we are seeing would be questionable or just generally bad if they were to happen in real life. In role-play, the role players are able to take on the personas of characters who likely differ from themselves and infer on what that character, with all of the knowledge of the character, with all of the assumptions that have been made about their actions and mannerisms, make decisions based off of the characters’ that they are playing. It is a good story such as that of DnDad which lets we, the audience, to also do the same, we can relate to the characters, and critique their actions, we then feel a great, somewhat personal connection to one of the characters.

For example, oh and spoiler warning for the rest of this document [I recommend that only if you have listened to the entirety of DnDad up to episode forty-nine that you continue reading this; unless you are the kind of person to not mind spoilers, cheers to you, continue on], the PC that was found to be a bad father, was the character Glenn Close, who was known to being a rockstar guitarist, being an open consumer of marijuana, and single father, which that latter has passed to his son, Nicholas “Nick” Close. When Glenn was made the defendant of a court case against him, with the charge of being a bad father [which apparently is considered against the law, but with more context one would be able to understand what was happening in the story]. The three other PCs, Henry Oak, Ron Stampler, Darryl Wilson, were essentially three lawyers with the goal of convincing the twelve real-life people who were role playing as jurors outside of the normal conversation of the podcast that Glenn was not a bad father, of which they had used many arguments based on the PCs’ knowledge of their relationships with Glenn, and the PCs’ personal view of Glenn as a person. I think in particularly, Beth May, who plays Ron Stampler, did very well in her characterization of Ron during the court case as it seems that he sits somewhere near the milder end of the autism spectrum, but May’s interpretation of Ron’s appraisal of the situation and May’s choice of words for Ron fits very well into what we expect of the character: a little bit insensitive, not so much as in a disrespectful kind of way, but perhaps as more as not understanding that he should be watering down the truthful description of Glenn as a person, when the others would most likely understate any of the bad things that Glenn has done.

As a listener to the excellent podcast that is DnDad, I was obviously somewhat aggravated when the team at DnDad left us all on a cliff-hanger at the end of 2020. Its was as if I was reading a book and someone snatched the book from my grasp whilst turning the page from the climax to the falling action, and being angry that they would keep the story from me. Writing this, I hear this excellent song from my first quarter of 2021 playlist; I think that that song would be a good sonic-metaphor for the chaos that is my mind right now considering what has occurred in episode forty-nine of DnDad. I feel that it would be relevant to bring up something I remember from my research on William Godwin; Godwin, in essay five of part one of his book “The Enquirer”, while talking about developing a liking of reading early in children, mentions that “The late reader makes a superficial acquaintance with his author, but is never admitted into the familiarity of a friend.” which would suggest that those do have an early liking of reading, do have a great intimate connection to the author and perhaps the product of the author. The sentences following that quote actually support that last point, “...the familiarity of a friend. Stiffness and formality are always visible between them. He does not become the creature of his author; neither bends with all his caprices, nor the sympathies with all his sensations.” Perhaps for books this is true, but I think otherwise for this improvisational work done by role players; like it feels to be listened to as if it were some piece of text, but then we remember that the story is entirely improvisational of some people, some people who are people in the real world just like us, some people who have full control of the characters of the story. With this we are able to juxtapose ourselves to the characters of the story, especially if the dictation and characterization of the characters is done very well. One statement that I have heard from someone who is involved with theater and theatrical performances is that the person playing the role must be able to lock in with the persona of the character to be able to do it well, which I think that the team at DnDad are doing well.

Part 2:

Stories are just recollections of events which are conveyed in a variety of ways, whether by hear-say, written as a book or series of texts, as a movie or series of videos televised or streamed on the internet today. I wanted to connect the stories told in books to those told in television shows, but I was having a hard time thinking of how this might be. I now remember how many Spanish speaking countries refer to their televised serial shows as “Telenovelas” which essentially means “televised novels” which I think places highlight on the differences between people acting out a story’s plot in a recorded video as the characters of the story would, versus how books or texts simply describe to us how the actions occur, and how the characters move about. Now, I can understand how Godwin would say that books require a supporting love for reading to fully process the information in a book as it would be simple to watch a person do something versus the somewhat more difficult task of understanding someone’s verbal/written description of what has occurred [yes, I know that Godwin being of the late eighteenth century did not have television, but he did have plays, which I would say fits the same position of people acting out characters]. For me, this still doesn’t explain why I feel such a great connection to the DnDad podcast.

I have been approaching this topic under the impression that Godwin’s idea of not being fully embraced into the written story as a result of not having an early love for reading was not compatible with the video or visual novel, but perhaps I was too quick to accept that idea. I know that from a young age that I have been interested in televised shows, namely the filipino shows that my family has watched in the many years prior before we had our cable t.v. removed about a year ago. I know that I have been able to enjoy and understand the shows with translation from family members and later, english captions.
