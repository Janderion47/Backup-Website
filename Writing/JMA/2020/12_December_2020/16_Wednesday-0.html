Post-Philosophy Thoughts:

Addiction to the Internet?

What was the original purpose of the internet? Well at first, it was used by the military so they could send commands and information quickly. Then later on, scientists used it to move information and data, and converse on it. In the present day, anyone can use the internet to communicate, send photographs, watch videos, watch for entertainment, read the news. Online News sources are not much different than their physical world counterparts, journalists are just as biased online as they are in a physical newspaper. Musicians can much more easily spread their music, and gain traction in the vast field of music lovers. Social Media platforms permit, well, media, to be spread around much more easily; your cool aunt from your mother’s side can now easily send you what is bascially a greeting card in an email. The internet was created to make the transfering of information much faster.

I think one of the easier topics to bring up related to this is the rise of an addiction to things related to the internet, lets say more specifically, an addiction to one’s phone. To understand why this would exist, we first have to know how the addiction starts in the first place. Now I have not done any scientific reseach, or have attempted to look online for any, but considering what I have seen of people my age, and what I have observed on the internet itself in this present day. I think I have a good idea of why people have an addiction to the internet.



Its important that we have a definition for addiction; an addiction is, as according to Oxford Languages, is “the fact or condition of being addicted to a particular substance, thing, or activity.”, which is uses the word within the definition, so… addicted, as according to Oxford Languages, is being “physically and mentally dependent on a particular substance, and unable to stop taking it without incurring adverse effects.” which seems to be in reference to substance or drug addiction which is probably much worse than any internet addiction, but we can see that addiction is a great dependence on something external to one’s self.

People want to be constantly connected to others online, so what motivation lies beneath this desire? I know that I have used the internet more in this time of social distancing and quarantine than before, and I have recognized this as a loneliness, from being detached from my friends and peers, an attempt to keep the bonds between each other from breaking or deteriorating over time. I was about to say that I do not believe that I have an addiction to the internet at the end of the previous sentence, but I know that between 06:10 and 06:50 every morning before school, I am watching a video on Youtube to entertain myself as I make my daily cup of coffee. I know my purpose of watching the videos is to be entertained, but does it that mean it is an addiction to the internet? If there were other options for entertainment in my daily routine, I do not know whether I would or would not choose them, as when I find a video in the morning I always have some preference; this feels similar to the following situation:

If I have a favorite musical band, for example, “Sungazer”, a band of Bassist Adam Neely, and Drummer Shawn Crowder, and I listen to them all the time, that would suggest that I have an addiction to their music; the fact that I use the music streaming service “Spotify” to listen to them does not mean I have an addiction to the service, as I could easily listen to Sungazer on Youtube, or Apple music, or Amazon Prime’s Music.
Or as another example, if you like the work of a specific author of books, you might read a lot of their work. One person may look at you and assume you have a addiction of books, but in reality, you do not, you love the work of a specific author.

I do not think that people have addictions to the internet itself, but to the content that is present on the platform. I know that one of my fellow students of philosophy has recognized that they have this large tendency of picking up their phone every morning after waking up, that means that this specific peer of mine has something on their phone that is something they are addicted to.

Also, is it an addiction to something if you have no other option than to use it? Not many retail stores sell vinyl record or casette tape players anymore, so does a person have an addiction to the use of online music services? One would have to be identified as listenting to music a lot, but sure you could say that this person has an addiction to listening to music, otherwise they would likely just like some music, but it does not seem that the repeated use of an online music source is a sufficient condition for having an addiction to online music services.



On a different note, I have once heard that some psychologists have done some research on people who seem to open their phone all the time. If I remember correctly, they found that people who regularly used social media, developed an anxiety about searching for updates on their social media feed, and have to keep checking their social media as to alleve that anxiety, which does not surprise me, which is something to make note of.

Corruption in Social Media Platforms

It has been revealed in recent years that outside forces, and even the leaders of the social media platforms have used their position to influence the thoughts of the people that use their platforms. This infringes on the people’s right to free thought. Regardless of the purpose of this, this is bad.

How would one attempt to draw someone away from the poor influence and stream of ideas presented on these social media platforms, as to reveal to them what is true and what is not? I think this problem is similar to the Allegory of the Cave, as we who are the ones who have left the cave, will come back, but it is not garunteed that our former fellow-prisoners will eventually leave the cave. We don’t need to convince them of that the place that they are in is a prison, we just need to teach them that there are other ways of thinking, that is what bringing someone out of the prison is; when the former prisoner looks back on the cave, they then know that the prison was a prison. We need to lead people to be able to critique practically anything, that is what the allegory says we should do.
